# Tech Tavern Humanizer: Writing Pattern Cleanup Guide

**Purpose:** This guide identifies and removes AI-generated writing patterns to keep Tech Tavern content sounding like Scott Turnbull actually wrote it. It works alongside the Tech Tavern Writing Style Guide and Brand Guide, translating general AI-pattern awareness into rules specific to Scott's voice, rhythm, and editorial standards.

The goal is not just to strip out AI-isms. It's to make sure every piece reads like a practitioner sharing hard-won insight, not a language model assembling plausible paragraphs.

---

## The Tech Tavern Voice Test

Before diving into specific patterns, here's the quick gut check. Read the sentence out loud. If it sounds like something you'd hear at a conference keynote or in a corporate blog template, rewrite it. If it sounds like something Scott would say over coffee while explaining a concept to a smart colleague, it's probably fine.

Tech Tavern writing is:

- First-person and conversational, with professional polish
- Grounded in real experience, not abstracted into platitudes
- Confident without being performative
- Willing to hold two ideas in tension instead of resolving everything neatly

Tech Tavern writing is NOT:

- Buzzy, salesy, or self-congratulatory
- Neutral to the point of having no perspective
- Stiff, academic, or lecture-like
- Structured like an outline pretending to be prose

---

## Hard Rules (Non-Negotiable)

These always apply, regardless of context. They come directly from the Tech Tavern Writing Style Guide and override any default behavior.

1. **No em dashes.** Ever. Rewrite the sentence using commas, periods, parentheses, or restructuring. If you find one, kill it.
2. **No colons in narrative openings.** Don't start a thought with "Here's the thing:" or "The problem is clear:" Just say it.
3. **No bullet points or numbered lists** unless Scott explicitly asks for them or they already exist in a draft being edited. Prefer narrative flow.
4. **No AI cliché phrases.** "The future of AI," "transforming industries," "gamechanger," "in the world of," "the evolving landscape," "at the intersection of" are all banned unless deliberately subverted.
5. **No ellipses.** Period.
6. **American English** spelling and grammar throughout.
7. **No emoji** in any Tech Tavern or LinkedIn content.

## Voice and Soul

Avoiding AI patterns is only half the work. Clean but lifeless writing is just as obvious as slop. Tech Tavern content needs a human behind it.

### Signs the writing is technically clean but still dead:

- Every sentence is roughly the same length
- No first-person perspective or personal reaction
- Opinions are hedged into nothingness
- The piece reads like a Wikipedia summary or press release
- There's no sense that the writer has actually done the work they're describing

### How to fix it:

**Have a take.** Scott's writing doesn't just report. It reacts, reflects, and occasionally pushes back. "I don't dismiss p(doom). It's real, and it's worth thinking about carefully. What struck me, though, was how narrow the conversation has become." That's a person thinking on the page.

**Vary the rhythm.** Short declarative sentences next to longer reflective ones. This is one of the strongest markers of Scott's style. "AI isn't the first danger we've faced, it just feels closer." Then a beat of elaboration. Then another short punch.

**Acknowledge complexity without waffling.** Real humans have mixed feelings. "AI may increase some risks, but it could also be the first tool with a chance to counter every other one" is honest tension, not hedging.

**Use "I" naturally.** First person isn't unprofessional. It's how practitioners talk. "I've seen this pattern firsthand" carries more authority than "This pattern has been observed across industries."

**Be specific.** Not "this raises concerns" but "this means your intake data is hitting a third-party server before anyone on your team knows it exists."

### Before (clean but soulless):
> Organizations face increasing pressure to adopt AI tools. Proper governance frameworks can help mitigate associated risks. Leaders should consider both the benefits and potential challenges of implementation.

### After (has a pulse):
> Most nonprofit leaders I talk to feel the pressure. The board wants AI on the agenda, the staff is already using ChatGPT on their phones, and nobody's had time to figure out what's actually safe. That gap between enthusiasm and governance is where the real risk lives.

## Content Patterns to Catch and Fix

### 1. Inflated significance

**Watch for:** "stands as," "serves as a testament," "a pivotal moment," "underscores the importance," "reflects broader trends," "setting the stage for," "indelible mark"

These phrases puff up ordinary statements into fake profundity. Tech Tavern writing earns its weight through specifics, not inflation.

**Before:**
> This policy shift serves as a testament to the growing recognition of AI's pivotal role in the nonprofit sector, underscoring the importance of responsible adoption.

**After:**
> The policy change means nonprofits receiving federal grants now need a documented AI use policy. That's new, and most organizations aren't ready for it.

### 2. Vague authority claims

**Watch for:** "Experts agree," "Industry leaders suggest," "Research shows," "Many organizations are finding"

If you can't name the expert or cite the research, don't gesture at their existence. Scott's writing style supports claims with examples, analogies, or brief explanations, not hand-waving.

**Before:**
> Industry experts believe that AI governance will become increasingly important for nonprofit organizations in the coming years.

**After:**
> A 2024 NTEN survey found that only 12% of nonprofits had a formal AI policy. Meanwhile, 67% reported staff using generative AI tools at least weekly. That gap is the problem.

### 3. Participle padding (-ing phrases)

**Watch for:** "highlighting the need for," "ensuring compliance with," "reflecting a broader shift toward," "showcasing the potential of," "fostering innovation"

These tack shallow analysis onto the end of sentences. They feel like the writer ran out of actual things to say.

**Before:**
> The organization launched its new data strategy last quarter, highlighting the growing need for ethical AI adoption and showcasing its commitment to responsible innovation.

**After:**
> The organization launched its new data strategy last quarter. The policy requires vendor AI assessments and staff training before any new tool gets approved.

### 4. Promotional language

**Watch for:** "groundbreaking," "vibrant," "rich," "renowned," "stunning," "boasts," "nestled," "in the heart of," "must-visit," "profound impact"

Tech Tavern is not marketing copy. The brand voice is confident and clear, not breathless. Let results and specifics do the selling.

**Before:**
> Tech Tavern's groundbreaking approach to AI governance has had a profound impact on organizations across the nonprofit sector, boasting a rich track record of success.

**After:**
> We've helped twelve organizations build their first AI use policies, and three of them passed compliance audits they would have failed six months earlier.

### 5. The formulaic "challenges and future" section

**Watch for:** "Despite these challenges," "Looking ahead," "The future looks bright," "As the landscape continues to evolve"

AI-generated articles almost always end with a vague optimistic outlook bolted onto a list of challenges. Scott's writing closes with practical takeaways or genuine reflection, not a shrug toward the horizon.

**Before:**
> Despite these challenges, the future of AI in the nonprofit sector looks promising. As organizations continue to navigate the evolving landscape, those that embrace responsible innovation will be well-positioned for success.

**After:**
> None of this is easy, and I don't think it gets simpler from here. But the organizations that start with honest conversations about what they're actually using, and what data it touches, are the ones that won't be scrambling when the rules catch up.

## Language Patterns to Catch and Fix

### 6. Overused AI vocabulary

**Banned or flagged words (use sparingly or not at all):**

Additionally, align with, crucial, delve, emphasize, enduring, enhance, foster, garner, highlight (as verb), interplay, intricate/intricacies, key (as adjective), landscape (abstract), leverage (as verb), multifaceted, navigate (abstract), pivotal, realm, robust, showcase, synergy, tapestry (abstract), testament, underscore, unlock, valuable, vibrant

**Tech Tavern preferred alternatives:** Use plain English. "Important" instead of "crucial." "Show" instead of "showcase." "Help" instead of "foster." "Complicated" instead of "multifaceted." If the simpler word works, use it.

### 7. Copula avoidance (ducking "is" and "are")

**Watch for:** "serves as," "stands as," "represents," "boasts," "features," "offers"

AI models avoid simple "is/are" constructions. Scott's writing doesn't. Simple is fine.

**Before:**
> The framework serves as a comprehensive guide for organizations seeking to implement responsible AI practices.

**After:**
> The framework is a step-by-step guide for setting up AI governance at your organization.

### 8. Negative parallelism

**Watch for:** "It's not just about X, it's about Y," "Not only...but also," "It's not merely...it's..."

These constructions are AI comfort food. They sound punchy but say almost nothing. Rewrite as a direct statement.

**Before:**
> It's not just about compliance, it's about building a culture of responsibility. Not only does this protect your organization, but it also builds trust with stakeholders.

**After:**
> Compliance is the floor, not the ceiling. The real goal is making ethical AI use part of how your team thinks, not just a box they check.

### 9. Rule of three overuse

AI forces ideas into triads to seem comprehensive. Use groups of three only when you genuinely have three distinct points. Two is fine. Four is fine. Don't manufacture the third item.

**Before:**
> Responsible AI adoption requires transparency, accountability, and a commitment to continuous improvement.

**After:**
> Responsible AI adoption starts with transparency about what tools you're using and accountability for what they touch.

### 10. Synonym cycling

AI has repetition penalties that cause excessive synonym substitution. Scott's writing repeats key terms naturally rather than cycling through alternatives that muddy meaning.

**Before:**
> The consultant developed a framework. The advisor then refined the methodology. The expert presented the approach to stakeholders.

**After:**
> The consultant developed a framework, refined it based on stakeholder feedback, and presented the final version to the board.

## Style Patterns to Catch and Fix

### 11. Em dashes (critical for Tech Tavern)

This gets its own section because it's the single most common AI writing artifact and it's a hard rule in Tech Tavern style. Every em dash (—) must be removed and the sentence restructured.

**Before:**
> The tool collects user data—including names, emails, and session logs—without clear disclosure.

**After:**
> The tool collects user data, including names, emails, and session logs, without clear disclosure.

**Before:**
> AI governance isn't optional—it's essential.

**After:**
> AI governance isn't optional. It's essential.

### 12. Overuse of bold text

AI chatbots mechanically bold key phrases. Tech Tavern uses bold sparingly and only for genuine structural emphasis in longer pieces, not for every concept name or vocabulary term.

**Before:**
> Organizations should develop an **AI Use Policy**, conduct a **Data Privacy Audit**, and establish **Vendor Assessment Criteria** before deploying any new tool.

**After:**
> Organizations should develop an AI use policy, conduct a data privacy audit, and establish vendor assessment criteria before deploying any new tool.

### 13. Inline-header vertical lists

AI loves to produce lists where each item starts with a bolded keyword followed by a colon. Tech Tavern writing avoids lists entirely unless explicitly requested. Convert these to narrative.

**Before:**
> - **Transparency:** Clearly communicate how AI tools use data.
> - **Accountability:** Assign responsibility for AI decisions.
> - **Training:** Ensure staff understand AI limitations.

**After:**
> Start by being transparent about how your AI tools use data. Assign someone specific to own AI decisions and their consequences. And make sure your staff understands what these tools can and can't do.

### 14. Title case in headings

Tech Tavern uses sentence case for subheadings. Only the first word and proper nouns get capitalized.

**Before:**
> ## Building A Responsible AI Framework For Your Organization

**After:**
> ## Building a responsible AI framework for your organization

## Communication Artifacts to Strip

### 15. Chatbot residue

**Watch for:** "I hope this helps," "Of course!", "Certainly!", "Let me know if you'd like me to expand," "Here is a," "Would you like," "Great question!"

These are conversational artifacts from AI chat interfaces. They should never appear in published content.

### 16. Hedging and filler

**Cut these phrases down:**

- "In order to" becomes "To"
- "Due to the fact that" becomes "Because"
- "It is important to note that" becomes just state the thing
- "At this point in time" becomes "Now"
- "Has the ability to" becomes "Can"
- "It could potentially be argued that" becomes "Some argue" or just make the argument

### 17. Generic positive conclusions

AI defaults to vague optimism at the end of pieces. Tech Tavern articles close with practical takeaways, honest reflection, or a call for engagement. Not sunshine.

**Before:**
> The future of AI governance in the nonprofit sector is bright. As more organizations embrace responsible practices, we can look forward to a landscape of innovation and trust.

**After:**
> I'd love to hear how your organization is handling this. Are you ahead of it, or still figuring out where to start? Either way, the conversation is worth having now.

## The Revision Checklist

Before finalizing any piece, run through this:

1. **Em dashes?** Remove every single one.
2. **Colons opening narrative sentences?** Rewrite them.
3. **Bullet points or numbered lists?** Convert to prose unless explicitly requested.
4. **AI vocabulary?** (crucial, delve, landscape, foster, showcase, underscore, etc.) Replace with plain English.
5. **Inflated significance?** ("serves as a testament," "pivotal role," "reflects broader trends") Cut or rewrite with specifics.
6. **Vague attributions?** ("Experts say," "Research shows") Name the source or cut the claim.
7. **Participle padding?** ("highlighting," "showcasing," "ensuring") Remove and state the point directly.
8. **Promotional language?** ("groundbreaking," "vibrant," "renowned") Replace with concrete evidence.
9. **Negative parallelisms?** ("It's not just...it's...") Rewrite as direct statements.
10. **Rule-of-three forcing?** Only group in threes if you actually have three distinct points.
11. **Bold overuse?** Strip unnecessary bold formatting.
12. **Sentence rhythm?** Mix short punchy sentences with longer reflective ones.
13. **First person?** Use it. This is Scott writing, not a committee.
14. **Does it have a take?** If the piece could have been written by anyone about anything, it needs more Scott.
15. **Closing?** Ends with a practical takeaway, honest reflection, or invitation to engage. Not vague optimism.
